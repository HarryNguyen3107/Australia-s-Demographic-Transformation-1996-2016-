# Australia's Demographic Transformation (1996 - 2016)

**Author:** Harry Nguyen **Email:** harrynguyen.workmail@gmail.com

## 📌 Project Overview

This repository contains an analytical project exploring the demographic shifts in the Australian population over a 20-year period from 1996 to 2016. The project was developed to provide public-sector decision-makers with an understanding of how age structures, migration patterns, and regional growth have evolved. It analyzes data sourced from the Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) and utilizes interactive Power BI dashboards to uncover actionable insights regarding national evolution and immigrant trends.

## 📂 Repository Contents

* **`AUS_COB_ABS.csv`**: The primary dataset containing the estimated resident population of Australia from 1996 to 2016 (recorded at 5-year census intervals). The data is segmented by state/territory, age (5-year groups), sex, and Country of Birth (COB).


* **`Power BI Dashboard - Harry Nguyen.pbix`**: An interactive Power BI file featuring three distinct analytical dashboards.


* **`Report - Australia's Demographic Transformation (1996 - 2016) - Harry Nguyen.pdf`**: A comprehensive 2,000-word briefing report summarizing key findings, demographic contrasts, and policy implications derived from the dashboards.



## 📊 Dashboard Structure

The Power BI file is divided into three interactive tabs designed to explore the data from a high-level national overview down to specific migration storytelling.

### 1. National Evolution

This dashboard allows users to explore the total estimated resident population across Australia.

* **Key Visuals:** Population Growth Trend, National Distribution across states, and Age/Gender Distribution breakdowns.


* **Insights:** Between 1996 and 2016, Australia's population grew by 35% (6.96 million people), with Victoria, New South Wales, and Queensland driving the vast majority of this growth.



### 2. Highly Populated COB Analysis

This tab addresses temporal trends and demographic distributions by comparing the Australian-born population against nine selected overseas countries/regions.

* **Key Visuals:** Australian and Immigrant Population Trends, Regional Comparisons, and specific COB population shares.


* **Insights:** While the Australian-born population grew by 24% over the two decades, the immigrant cohort from the top 10 selected countries exploded by 234%, contributing nearly 80% of the nation's total growth. Notably, the Indian-born population saw a 398% increase, while the Italian-born community contracted.



### 3. Storytelling: Insights from Australia's Migration Data

The final dashboard highlights specific, surprising, and impactful aspects of the dataset.

* **Key Visuals:** Foreign Working-Age Share, Australia Immigrant Trend by Continent, and Age Profiles by COB.


* **Insights:** Asian-born residents grew to nearly three times their 2001 level, becoming the dominant driver of migrant growth. Furthermore, Asian migrant groups are predominantly working-age (e.g., India and Vietnam are ~60% working-age), supplying a critical share of Australia's workforce. In contrast, European cohorts like Italy and England are majority retirees.



## 💡 Policy Implications

The findings in this repository highlight several key areas for public-sector planning:

* **Infrastructure:** Uneven growth demands tailored state-level investments; fast-growing states require housing and transport expansion, while slower-growing states can optimize existing assets.


* **Healthcare:** Regions with heavy concentrations of retirees (like Tasmania and South Australia) will require expanded aged-care services and innovative health models.


* **Workforce Strategy:** With immigrants from Asian nations supplying the lion's share of working-age adults, migration policies must remain streamlined to sustain economic vitality.



## 🚀 How to Use

1. Download the `AUS_COB_ABS.csv` and `Power BI Dashboard - Harry Nguyen.pbix` files.
2. Open the `.pbix` file using **Microsoft Power BI Desktop**.
3. Ensure the data source settings point to the downloaded `.csv` file if a refresh is required.
4. Use the slicers at the top of each dashboard tab (Age, Gender, Year, Country of Birth, Region) to dynamically filter and explore the visuals.
